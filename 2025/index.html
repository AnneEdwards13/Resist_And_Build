    Venues

    Main Venue: The MURPH, 561 W Whitehall St SW, Atlanta, GA 30310

    The Multi-Use Radical People’s Hall (MURPH) is 9,700 sq. ft. building in Atlanta’s historic West End community. The MURPH is a space for individuals, organizations, groups and communities to gather, work, dream, scheme and take action for our collective liberation, rooted in community, radical feminist, and anti-capitalist values. Video of the space.

    Like many communities with deep Black cultural roots and institutions, the West End is experiencing rapid gentrification and loss of communally held spaces. The MURPH builds on the community’s abundant legacy to offer affordable, accessible and well equipped meeting space.

    The MURPH is also part of the Community Stewardship Trust (CST), a community-owned real estate project incubated by The Guild. The CST brings together local residents to co-own and steward properties —like affordable housing, grocery stores, or spaces for small businesses and organizations. It’s a real estate model that builds community wealth and supports residents to thrive in place. Book the MURPH for your Atlanta event.

    Getting there: The closest MARTA station is the West End Station (8 minute walk).

    Parking: During the day, guests are encouraged to park at the West End MARTA station parking lot. In the evening, guests can park at the Footlocker just behind The MURPH. Please reserve parking at The MURPH for Summit staff and guests with access needs.

    Access: The building is one level (no stairs) and includes an accessible entrance.

    Workshop Venue: Morehouse Leadership Center, Westview Dr SW & West End Ave SW, Atlanta, GA 30314

    Getting there: Morehouse Leadership Center is a 10-minute walk from the main venue and a 14-minute walk from the West End MARTA station.

    Parking:

    • A paid parking garage on campus is $8/day or $3 for less than 2 hours. It’s a 5-minute walk from the Center.
    • There is free street parking on Westview Drive, the street directly behind Morehouse College, off Joseph Lowery.
    • There are city parking meters on other streets surrounding Morehouse College.

    Carnival Venue: The Wren’s Nest, 1050 Ralph David Abernathy Blvd SW, Atlanta, GA 30310

    The Wren’s Nest is a cultural center, house museum, and National Historic Landmark in Atlanta’s West End neighborhood.

    Getting there: The closest MARTA station is the West End Station (15 minute walk)

    Health and Accessibility

    Our COVID Safety Guidelines:

    • All attendees must wear masks indoors
    • All attendees must mask while traveling
    • All attendees must rapid test each morning
    • All attendees must bring sufficient rapid tests and masks for themselves
    • All attendees must stay home if feeling unwell
    • All attendees are encouraged to get a COVID vaccine booster shot at least 4-6 weeks prior to the summit

    • HEPA air purifiers in indoor spaces
    • Ventilation via open doors/windows when possible
    • Audio amplification equipment
    • Gender-neutral bathrooms
    • Fragrance-free spaces
    • Designated quiet/healing areas

    • Pre-event access needs assessment

    Note: The specific features we can offer will depend on our fundraising success and final registration numbers. We are committed to maximizing accessibility within our resources and will provide regular updates as we confirm which features we can definitively provide.

    Strategic Objectives

    Movement Priorities and Goals

    In the current context, our priorities are to:

    • Strengthen networks and trust across the solidarity economy and systems change movements, particularly centering leadership from Southern, Black, Indigenous, and marginalized communities globally.
    • Enrich and expand our movements, making them irresistible by fostering broad alignment around systemic change. Build alliances that overcome fragmentation, moving together on common ground to scale movements toward transformative change.
    • Deepen our collective vision and strategies for liberation from the interlocking systems of oppression, extraction, and domination. This includes cultivating shared strategies to address capitalist exploitation, colonialism, ecological harm, and inequality, with the ultimate goal of achieving systemic transformation.
    • Draw from the visionary traditions of BIPOC and other oppressed and exploited peoples to fight for freedom, create community-driven solutions, and reconnect with the land and nonhuman kin, which offer essential inspiration for building the regenerative, just futures we seek.
    • Foster a joyful, regenerative culture that embodies the values of justice, interdependence, and ecological restoration, emphasizing care, mutual aid, and collective healing. Draw insights from feminisms, disability justice, and solidarity frameworks to prioritize ongoing individual and collective well-being.

    Summit Strategic Orientation and Goals

    The 2025 Summit seeks to achieve the following objectives, in alignment with the broader movement priorities and Resist & Build network goals:

    • Create an in-person opportunity for connection and community-building within the Resist & Build network and beyond, centering the leadership of marginalized communities globally.
    • Leverage Atlanta’s rich legacy of Black resistance, civil rights, and solidarity organizing to catalyze a new phase of the global movement for systemic change, aligned with struggles against U.S. imperialism and corporate extraction in the Global South.
    • Showcase ongoing resistance efforts (e.g., Stop Cop Nation, solidarity with Palestine, etc.) and solidarity economy initiatives (e.g. Community real estate, mutual aid, etc.), amplifying their interconnections.
    • Collectively strategize around the key priorities of the movement, with a focus on the unique challenges and opportunities of the Southern context and its links to global dynamics.
    • Engage in rigorous dialogues to analyze the systemic roots of intersecting crises, including capitalist imperialism, colonialism, ecological devastation, and the rise of fascism, centering a global, intersectional perspective. Clarify shared and divergent understandings of “systems change,” post-capitalism, and non-reformism. 
    • Learn from the visionary traditions of BIPOC and other oppressed and exploited peoples to inform our struggles for liberation, community-driven solutions, and reconnection with the land and nonhuman kin, inspiring summit participants to embody regenerative and just futures in their organizing and everyday practices.
    • Develop collective strategies for non-reformist, post-capitalist, and/or post-colonial alternatives, with a vision for dismantling capitalism and building regenerative alternatives.
    • Catalyze new collaborations and projects that can advance movement goals, identifying specific, actionable paths for building long-term infrastructure to sustain collaboration.
    • Cultivate a transformative culture that inspires, empowers, and embodies the values of liberation, cooperation, and ecological restoration. Prioritize practices of care, mutual aid, and interdependence that nourish movement resilience and personal well-being.
    • Strengthen Atlanta’s solidarity economy ecosystem, and strengthen connections to national and global movements for systemic change.
    • Establish pathways for ongoing coordination and collaboration within the Resist & Build network to sustain momentum post-summit.

    Collaborative Planning Approach

    A joint effort between the local host circle, national Backbone Circle, and Summit Planning Circle focusing on key collaboration areas:

    • Venue selection and logistics
    • Local outreach and attendee engagement
    • Program development
    • “Carnival Beyond Capitalism” festival
    • Volunteer and vendor coordination

    The national team provides support and guidance, while the local committee offers Atlanta-specific insights and networks.

    Why Atlanta?

    One of our Summit Goals is to leverage Atlanta’s rich legacy of Black resistance, civil rights, and solidarity organizing to catalyze a new phase of the global movement for systemic change, aligned with struggles against U.S. imperialism and corporate extraction in the Global South.

    • Rich civil rights history
    • Ongoing resistance (e.g., against “Cop City”, solidarity with Palestine)
    • Thriving solidarity economy organizing (OHRD, EndStateATL, The Guild, AFSC, Housing Justice League, Community Movement Builders, Project South, and more)
    • Southern context: “As goes the South, so goes the nation…”
    • Intersection of social, economic, and environmental justice movements
    • Major transport hub
